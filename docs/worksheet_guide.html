English Reading Worksheet · Grade 6

The Monkey King's Journey

Based on the classic Chinese tale of Sun Wukong
Part 1

📖 Reading Passage

Read the passage carefully before answering the questions below.

The Monkey King, Sun Wukong, was known across the heavens for his incredible powers. After causing great trouble—stealing a potion of immortality and defeating the Jade Emperor's strongest warriors—he was finally captured and locked in a fiery prison. But instead of weakening him, the heat only made him stronger. Desperate, the Jade Emperor turned to the only being more powerful than himself: the Buddha.

Sun Wukong refused to show respect even before the Buddha. Boasting of his abilities, he demanded to be made Emperor. The Buddha smiled and offered him a challenge: if the Monkey King could somersault out of his palm, he would grant the wish. Confident as always, Sun Wukong leapt into the air and flew to what he thought was the edge of the heavens—a place marked by five great pillars. He left his mark there and returned to claim his prize.

But the Buddha simply pointed down. Sun Wukong had never left the Buddha's hand at all. The "pillars" were the Buddha's five fingers. Before the Monkey King could react, those fingers became a mountain range and pinned him to the ground. There he remained for 500 years, growing more desperate with every passing century.

After five hundred years, a goddess named Guanyin passed by his prison. She was guiding a holy monk named Tripitaka, who was traveling west to retrieve sacred scriptures. The journey was long and dangerous, so Guanyin offered Sun Wukong a deal: serve as Tripitaka's faithful disciple, and she would set him free. The Monkey King agreed without hesitation.

Freedom, however, proved harder than he expected. When bandits attacked them, Sun Wukong's violent temper flared up. Tripitaka scolded him, and the embarrassed Monkey King ran away. But after reflecting on a wise dragon king's advice—that true enlightenment requires giving up one's ego—Sun Wukong returned to apologize.

Guanyin, not fully trusting him yet, had given Tripitaka a golden headband and a secret spell. When the Monkey King put on the beautiful cap that held the band, Tripitaka recited the spell and the golden ring squeezed tight around Sun Wukong's head. It caused him great pain whenever he lost control. At last, the Monkey King was ready—not just for the journey West, but perhaps for the journey toward becoming a better version of himself.

Part 2

📚 Key Vocabulary

Study these words from the passage before answering the questions.

immortality
noun
The ability to live forever and never die. Sun Wukong stole a potion of immortality.
somersault
verb / noun
A rolling jump where the body turns completely over in the air.
enlightenment
noun
A state of deep wisdom and peace, especially in Buddhist belief; understanding the truth about life.
disciple
noun
A student or follower who learns from and helps a teacher or leader.
ego
noun
A person's sense of their own importance; pride in oneself, sometimes too much pride.
scripture
noun
Sacred or holy writings that are considered very important in a religion.

Part 4

✍️ Short Essay

Prompt: In the story, Sun Wukong must learn to control his ego and temper in order to grow as a person. Think about a time when you or someone you know had to learn an important lesson about self-control or humility. Write a short essay (3–4 paragraphs) comparing that experience to Sun Wukong's journey. How is it similar? How is it different?
💡 Writing Tips Your essay should include: (1) an introduction that states your main idea, (2) body paragraphs with specific examples, and (3) a conclusion that wraps up your thoughts. Use transition words like similarly, however, just like, in contrast, as a result.

Q6 Model: The Dragon King told Sun Wukong that to attain enlightenment, he had to abandon his ego even if it was painful. This was important because it showed him that true growth required humility, not just power.

Q7 Model: At the start, Sun Wukong is arrogant and violent—he steals, fights, and runs away from responsibility. By the end, he accepts the golden headband, apologizes to Tripitaka, and commits to the journey, showing growth in self-control.

Q8 Open-ended: Accept reasonable responses. Key idea: Guanyin's doubt reflects Sun Wukong's long history of bad behavior. It shows that trust must be earned through actions over time, not just words.
